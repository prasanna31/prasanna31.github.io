Invariant Subspaces and Diagonalization

Invariant subspaces and diagonalization are central concepts in linear algebra. They allow us to simplify linear transformations by identifying subspaces that remain unchanged under the transformation, and represent matrices in a simpler, diagonal form using eigenvectors.

1. Invariant Subspaces

A subspace W ⊆ V is called invariant under a linear transformation T: V → V if applying T to any vector in W keeps it in W:

v ∈ W ⇒ T(v) ∈ W

Intuition: The transformation T "does not leave" the subspace W. Examples of invariant subspaces include:

  • The zero subspace {0} (trivial invariant subspace)
  • The entire space V
  • Eigenspaces corresponding to a particular eigenvalue

1.1 Properties

  • Intersection and sum of invariant subspaces are also invariant.
  • If V = W₁ ⊕ W₂ (direct sum) with W₁, W₂ invariant under T, then T can be analyzed separately on each Wᵢ.

2. Connection to Eigenvectors

Eigenvectors naturally generate invariant subspaces:

If T(v) = λ v, then E_λ = {α v | α ∈ F} is 1-dimensional invariant subspace.

More generally, the eigenspace corresponding to λ is an invariant subspace:

v ∈ E_λ ⇒ T(v) = λ v ∈ E_λ

Hence, eigenvectors and eigenspaces are the building blocks for invariant subspaces.

3. Diagonalization

A square matrix A (or linear transformation T) is diagonalizable if there exists a basis of V consisting of eigenvectors of A. In this basis, A has a diagonal representation:

A = P D P⁻¹

Where:

  • P = matrix whose columns are eigenvectors of A
  • D = diagonal matrix of corresponding eigenvalues

Intuition: Diagonalization transforms A into a simpler form where the action of A on eigenvectors is just scaling, making powers, exponentials, and other functions of A easy to compute.

3.1 Criteria for Diagonalization

A matrix A ∈ ℝⁿˣⁿ is diagonalizable if:

  • The sum of the dimensions of all eigenspaces equals n
  • Equivalently: there exist n linearly independent eigenvectors

Note: Distinct eigenvalues guarantee diagonalizability, but repeated eigenvalues require checking the geometric multiplicity.

4. Steps for Diagonalization

  1. Compute eigenvalues λ by solving det(A - λ I) = 0
  2. For each eigenvalue, find the corresponding eigenspace E_λ by solving (A - λ I)v = 0
  3. Check if the total number of independent eigenvectors = n
  4. If yes, form P with columns as eigenvectors and D with diagonal entries as eigenvalues
  5. Verify: A = P D P⁻¹

5. Examples

1. 2×2 diagonal matrix: A = [[2,0],[0,3]]
Eigenvectors: v₁ = [1,0], v₂ = [0,1]
Diagonalization: P = [[1,0],[0,1]], D = [[2,0],[0,3]] (already diagonal)
Eigenspaces E₂ = span{[1,0]}, E₃ = span{[0,1]} → invariant subspaces
2. 2×2 matrix with distinct eigenvalues: A = [[4,1],[2,3]]
Characteristic equation: det(A-λI) = (4-λ)(3-λ) - 2*1 = λ² -7λ +10 =0 → λ=5,2
Eigenvectors: v₁=[1,1] for λ=5, v₂=[-1,2] for λ=2
Form P=[v₁ v₂], D=diag(5,2), check A=PDP⁻¹

6. Geometric Intuition

  • Invariant subspaces are “fixed directions” or “planes” that remain within themselves under transformation.
  • Diagonalization decomposes the space into invariant subspaces along eigenvectors where the transformation acts as simple scaling.
  • Once diagonalized, computations like Aⁿ become easy: Aⁿ = P Dⁿ P⁻¹

7. Summary Formulas

Invariant subspace: W ⊆ V, T(W) ⊆ W
Eigenspace: E_λ = {v | (A - λ I)v = 0}, an invariant subspace
Diagonalization: A = P D P⁻¹, P = eigenvectors, D = diagonal eigenvalues
A diagonalizable ⇔ exists n independent eigenvectors

Invariant subspaces and diagonalization simplify the analysis of linear transformations. Eigenvectors span invariant subspaces, and diagonalization expresses the transformation in a coordinate system where its action is purely scaling.
