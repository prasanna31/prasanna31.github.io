Minimal and Characteristic Polynomials — Comprehensive Explanation

In linear algebra, understanding the deep structure of a linear transformation or matrix requires examining its eigenvalues, eigenvectors, invariant subspaces, and the polynomials that encode these properties. Two of the most central tools in this analysis are the characteristic polynomial and the minimal polynomial. They are not just abstract constructions; they provide concrete information about diagonalizability, matrix powers, functions of matrices, Jordan canonical forms, and the geometric behavior of transformations.

1. Characteristic Polynomial: Definition and Intuition

Let A ∈ Fⁿˣⁿ be a square matrix over a field F (for example, ℝ or ℂ). The characteristic polynomial of A is the polynomial defined as:

p_A(λ) = det(A - λ I)

Here, I is the identity matrix of the same size as A, and λ is an indeterminate. Geometrically, the characteristic polynomial encodes the eigenvalues of A: the values of λ for which there exists a nonzero vector v such that A v = λ v. In other words, it tells us for which scalars λ the transformation stretches vectors without changing their directions.

1.1 Properties of the Characteristic Polynomial

  • Degree = n, which is the size of the matrix A.
  • Leading coefficient = (-1)ⁿ.
  • The constant term = det(A).
  • The coefficient of λ^(n-1) = -trace(A), where trace(A) = sum of diagonal entries.
  • Roots of p_A(λ) are exactly the eigenvalues of A.
  • The characteristic polynomial is invariant under similarity transformations: if B = P⁻¹AP, then p_B(λ) = p_A(λ).

1.2 Example of Characteristic Polynomial

A = [[2, 1], [1, 2]]
Compute p_A(λ) = det([[2-λ, 1], [1, 2-λ]]) = (2-λ)(2-λ) - 1 = λ² - 4λ + 3
Eigenvalues: λ = 1, 3

This example illustrates that the characteristic polynomial encapsulates eigenvalues in its roots, and its coefficients provide trace and determinant information.

1.3 Further Insight

The characteristic polynomial tells us where "stretching directions" exist in a linear transformation. In 2D, eigenvectors are lines that remain invariant under the transformation. The characteristic polynomial encapsulates these directions algebraically. In higher dimensions, it tells us about invariant subspaces, scaling factors, and the overall spectrum of the transformation.

2. Minimal Polynomial: Definition and Motivation

The minimal polynomial of a matrix A, denoted m_A(x), is the monic polynomial of least degree such that:

m_A(A) = 0

This means substituting the matrix A into m_A(x) yields the zero matrix. The minimal polynomial captures the smallest algebraic constraint satisfied by the matrix. While the characteristic polynomial reveals eigenvalues, the minimal polynomial reveals the algebraic structure of the matrix: which powers and linear combinations of A ultimately annihilate it.

2.1 Key Properties of Minimal Polynomial

  • Unique and monic (leading coefficient = 1).
  • Degree ≤ n.
  • Every eigenvalue of A is a root of m_A(x).
  • m_A(x) divides the characteristic polynomial p_A(x).
  • If A is diagonalizable, m_A(x) has distinct linear factors corresponding to eigenvalues.
  • Repeated roots in m_A(x) indicate non-diagonalizable components, corresponding to Jordan blocks.

2.2 Intuition Behind the Minimal Polynomial

Think of the minimal polynomial as the “simplest algebraic instruction” that annihilates the matrix. While the characteristic polynomial may have repeated factors, the minimal polynomial tells us the exact multiplicity needed to zero out the matrix. For example, if A has a repeated eigenvalue λ but only one linearly independent eigenvector, then (A - λ I)² = 0, which the minimal polynomial captures.

2.3 Examples of Minimal Polynomials

A = [[2, 1], [0, 2]]
Characteristic polynomial: p_A(x) = (2-x)²
Minimal polynomial: m_A(x) = (2-x)²
Observation: A is not diagonalizable because the geometric multiplicity of λ=2 is 1 (fewer independent eigenvectors than multiplicity).
A = [[3,0], [0,4]]
Characteristic polynomial: p_A(x) = (3-x)(4-x)
Minimal polynomial: m_A(x) = (3-x)(4-x)
Observation: A is diagonalizable because the minimal polynomial has distinct linear factors.

3. Relationship Between Minimal and Characteristic Polynomials

The two polynomials are closely related but serve different purposes:

  • Characteristic polynomial: Encodes all eigenvalues and global matrix properties like trace and determinant.
  • Minimal polynomial: Encodes the “algebraic essence” of A — the smallest polynomial that annihilates the matrix.
  • The minimal polynomial divides the characteristic polynomial.
  • Repeated roots in the minimal polynomial indicate non-diagonalizable parts, i.e., Jordan blocks.

3.1 Example Illustrating the Relationship

A = [[5,1,0],[0,5,0],[0,0,3]]
Characteristic polynomial: p_A(x) = (5-x)²(3-x)
Eigenvalues: λ = 5 (double), λ = 3
Minimal polynomial: m_A(x) = (5-x)²(3-x) → repeated root indicates non-diagonalizable component
Observation: The minimal polynomial reveals the size of the Jordan block associated with λ=5.

4. Connection to Diagonalization

A matrix A is diagonalizable if and only if the minimal polynomial has only simple linear factors. In this case, the minimal polynomial is simply the product of (x - λ_i) for all distinct eigenvalues λ_i.

  • If m_A(x) has repeated factors, there are invariant subspaces of dimension greater than 1 that are not spanned by eigenvectors, preventing diagonalization.
  • The characteristic polynomial might have repeated roots, but the minimal polynomial tells us the exact algebraic multiplicity needed to zero out each part of the matrix.

4.1 Computation of Diagonalization Using Minimal Polynomial

To diagonalize A:

  1. Compute eigenvalues λ by solving det(A - λ I) = 0.
  2. Compute eigenspaces E_λ = {v | (A - λ I)v = 0}.
  3. Verify that the sum of dimensions of eigenspaces equals n.
  4. Construct P with independent eigenvectors and D with eigenvalues.
  5. Check A = P D P⁻¹.

If this fails, check minimal polynomial: repeated roots indicate non-diagonalizable blocks.

5. Geometric Interpretation

Minimal and characteristic polynomials have a geometric meaning:

  • Eigenvalues indicate scaling along invariant directions.
  • Characteristic polynomial provides all potential invariant directions and their multiplicities.
  • Minimal polynomial tells us which powers of A are needed to "annihilate" components along repeated eigenvalues, capturing Jordan structure.
  • Repeated roots in minimal polynomial indicate “generalized eigenvectors” forming chains, corresponding to higher-dimensional invariant subspaces.

6. Applications

Understanding these polynomials is critical for:

  • Diagonalization and matrix powers: Aⁿ = P Dⁿ P⁻¹.
  • Matrix exponentials in differential equations: e^{At} = P e^{Dt} P⁻¹.
  • Jordan canonical forms for non-diagonalizable matrices.
  • Analyzing stability in dynamical systems: eigenvalues and minimal polynomials indicate growth/decay rates.
  • Invariant subspaces and decomposition of vector spaces.

7. Summary Formulas

Characteristic polynomial: p_A(λ) = det(A - λ I), degree = n
Minimal polynomial: m_A(x), monic, least degree s.t. m_A(A) = 0
Eigenvalues = roots of p_A(x) = roots of m_A(x)
Minimal polynomial divides characteristic polynomial
Diagonalizable ⇔ m_A(x) has distinct linear factors

Minimal and characteristic polynomials are more than just algebraic constructs: they provide a unified framework for understanding the spectral and structural properties of matrices. From computing eigenvalues, analyzing invariant subspaces, determining diagonalizability, and understanding Jordan forms, these polynomials are essential in both theoretical and applied linear algebra.
