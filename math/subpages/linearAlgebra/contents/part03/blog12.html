Dimension as an Invariant

In linear algebra, the concept of dimension captures the "size" or "degree of freedom" of a vector space. More importantly, dimension is an invariant, meaning that it is a property of the vector space itself, independent of the choice of basis. Understanding dimension as an invariant is central to many areas of mathematics, including linear transformations, matrices, and abstract vector spaces.

1. What Is Dimension?

The dimension of a vector space V, denoted dim(V), is the number of vectors in any basis of V. This definition relies on two fundamental facts:

  1. Every basis of a finite-dimensional vector space has the same number of vectors.
  2. Every vector in the space can be uniquely represented as a linear combination of the basis vectors.

Dimension counts the minimum number of vectors needed to “span” the space without redundancy. It is both a numerical and structural property of the space.

Example 1.1: ℝ²

ℝ² has dimension 2. Any two independent vectors form a basis, e.g., {(1,0),(0,1)} or {(1,1),(1,-1)}. No set of three or more independent vectors exists in ℝ²; any set of two independent vectors spans the space.

Example 1.2: ℝ³

ℝ³ has dimension 3. Any three linearly independent vectors form a basis. Adding a fourth vector always introduces linear dependence.

2. Dimension Is an Invariant

Why do we call dimension an invariant? Because the number of vectors in any basis of a vector space is always the same, regardless of which basis you choose. This is a deep structural property of the vector space.

Theorem 2.1: All bases of a finite-dimensional vector space have the same number of vectors

Let B₁={v₁,...,vₘ} and B₂={w₁,...,wₙ} be two bases of a finite-dimensional vector space V. Then m=n.

Proof:

  1. Since B₁ is a basis, it spans V. Each wⱼ ∈ B₂ can be written as a linear combination of B₁.
  2. By a fundamental lemma of linear algebra (Steinitz Exchange Lemma), the number of vectors in any independent set ≤ number of vectors in any spanning set.
  3. Applying this lemma:
    • n ≤ m (B₂ is independent, B₁ spans)
    • m ≤ n (B₁ is independent, B₂ spans)
  4. Thus m = n.

✅ Hence, dimension is well-defined and invariant under the choice of basis.

Example 2.1

In ℝ³, the standard basis has three vectors {(1,0,0),(0,1,0),(0,0,1)}. Another basis {(1,1,0),(0,1,1),(1,0,1)} also has exactly three vectors. Dimension is invariant: dim(ℝ³)=3.

3. Geometric Intuition

Dimension measures “degrees of freedom”:

  • In ℝ¹, vectors lie along a line: 1D space.
  • In ℝ², vectors lie on a plane: 2D space.
  • In ℝ³, vectors lie in 3D space: three independent directions span the space.
  • In function spaces, dimension counts independent “directions” in infinite-dimensional spaces (e.g., polynomial degrees, Fourier modes).

4. Relation to Linear Independence and Span

Dimension is fundamentally tied to two concepts:

  1. Linear Independence: Dimension equals the maximum number of independent vectors in the space.
  2. Span: Dimension equals the minimum number of vectors needed to span the space.

In other words:

dim(V) = size of any basis = max{|S| : S ⊆ V independent} = min{|S| : S ⊆ V spans V}

Example 4.1

In ℝ², the set {(1,0),(0,1),(1,1)} is spanning but not independent (size 3). The set {(1,0),(0,1)} is both spanning and independent (size 2), giving the dimension.

5. Dimension of Subspaces

If W ⊆ V is a subspace, dim(W) ≤ dim(V). Equality occurs only if W = V.

Example 5.1

Consider the xy-plane in ℝ³: W = {(x,y,0)}. dim(W)=2 < dim(ℝ³)=3. Any basis of W (e.g., {(1,0,0),(0,1,0)}) has fewer vectors than any basis of ℝ³.

6. Dimension Formula for Sum of Subspaces

Let U,W ⊆ V be subspaces. Then:

dim(U + W) = dim(U) + dim(W) - dim(U ∩ W)

Proof:

Choose a basis of U ∩ W, extend it to bases of U and W. Combine them to form a basis of U+W. Counting vectors gives the formula.

Example 6.1

In ℝ³, let U = xy-plane, W = xz-plane. Then dim(U)=2, dim(W)=2, dim(U∩W)=1 (x-axis). Hence:

dim(U+W) = 2 + 2 - 1 = 3

Indeed, U+W = ℝ³.

7. Applications of Dimension as an Invariant

  • Linear Transformations: Rank-nullity theorem uses invariant dimension:
    • dim(V) = rank(T) + nullity(T)
  • Matrices: Column space and row space dimensions (rank) are invariant under basis change.
  • Coordinates: Changing basis preserves dimension but changes coordinates of vectors.
  • Function Spaces: Count degrees of freedom for solutions of differential equations.

Example 7.1

Let T: ℝ³ → ℝ², T(x,y,z) = (x+y, y+z). dim(ℝ³)=3, rank(T)=2, nullity(T)=1. Dimension invariance ensures rank + nullity = 3.

  3. Let U and W be subspaces of ℝ³ with dim(U)=2, dim(W)=2, dim(U∩W)=1. Find dim(U+W).

  5. Give two different bases of ℝ² and show that dimension is invariant.

9. Summary

Dimension is an invariant that captures the fundamental structure of vector spaces. It counts independent directions, measures degrees of freedom, and underlies linear transformations, matrices, and subspace theory. Understanding dimension as an invariant provides deep insights into vector spaces, ensuring consistency across bases, subspaces, and transformations. Mastery of this concept is crucial for both theoretical and applied linear algebra.
