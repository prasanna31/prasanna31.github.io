Change of Basis and Representation

In linear algebra, the concept of a basis allows us to represent vectors in terms of coordinates relative to that basis. Changing the basis gives us a new perspective on the vector space, and understanding how to represent vectors and linear transformations in different bases is crucial for applications ranging from computer graphics to quantum mechanics.

1. What is a Basis?

A basis of a vector space V is a set of vectors {v₁, v₂, ..., vₙ} that satisfy two properties:

  • Linearly independent: No vector in the set can be written as a combination of others.
  • Spanning: Every vector in V can be expressed as a linear combination of the basis vectors.
v = a₁v₁ + a₂v₂ + ... + aₙvₙ

The coefficients a₁, a₂, ..., aₙ are the coordinates of v in the basis {v₁,...,vₙ}.

2. Representation of a Vector in a Basis

If B = {v₁, ..., vₙ} is a basis of V, then for any vector v ∈ V, the coordinate vector of v with respect to B is:

[v]_B = \begin{bmatrix} a₁ \\ a₂ \\ ... \\ aₙ \end{bmatrix} \text{ such that } v = a₁v₁ + ... + aₙvₙ

Intuition: The coordinate vector [v]_B is a compact representation of v in terms of B. Changing the basis changes these coordinates.

Example:
V = ℝ², standard basis B = {e₁, e₂} with e₁ = (1,0), e₂ = (0,1)
v = (3,5)
Then [v]_B = [3,5]ᵀ
If we choose a new basis C = {u₁, u₂} with u₁ = (1,1), u₂ = (1,-1), coordinates of v change:
Solve v = c₁u₁ + c₂u₂ → c₁ = 4, c₂ = -1 → [v]_C = [4,-1]ᵀ

3. Change of Basis — Concept

Suppose we have two bases of V: B = {v₁,...,vₙ} and C = {u₁,...,uₙ}. The change of basis problem asks: given the coordinates of a vector v in basis B, how can we find its coordinates in basis C?

We introduce the change of basis matrix P:

[v]_C = P [v]_B

P is constructed by expressing the vectors of B in terms of the C basis:

P = \begin{bmatrix} [v₁]_C & [v₂]_C & ... & [vₙ]_C \end{bmatrix}

Each column of P is the coordinate vector of a basis vector of B expressed in terms of C.

4. Example of Change of Basis

V = ℝ², B = {v₁=(1,0), v₂=(0,1)}, C = {u₁=(1,1), u₂=(1,-1)}
Coordinates of B vectors in C:
v₁ = 0.5 u₁ + 0.5 u₂ → [v₁]_C = [0.5,0.5]ᵀ
v₂ = 0.5 u₁ - 0.5 u₂ → [v₂]_C = [0.5,-0.5]ᵀ
Change of basis matrix P = [[0.5,0.5],[0.5,-0.5]]
Given [v]_B = [3,5]ᵀ → [v]_C = P [v]_B = [4,-1]ᵀ

5. Change of Basis for Linear Transformations

Let T: V → V be a linear transformation. Its matrix representation depends on the basis. Suppose [T]_B is the matrix of T in basis B, and [T]_C is the matrix in basis C. Then:

[T]_C = P [T]_B P⁻¹

Where P is the change of basis matrix from B to C.

Intuition: The matrix [T]_B encodes T relative to B. Changing the basis essentially rotates and scales our “coordinate axes,” and the conjugation formula adjusts the matrix accordingly while preserving the linear transformation’s action.

6. Geometric Interpretation

  • Think of B and C as two different coordinate grids on the same vector space.
  • Coordinates of vectors change, but the vectors themselves do not move.
  • Linear transformations look different in different bases, but the underlying mapping remains identical.
  • Diagonalization is a special case where we choose a basis in which the transformation is represented by a diagonal matrix — making computations easier.

7. Steps to Compute Change of Basis

  1. Choose old basis B = {v₁,...,vₙ} and new basis C = {u₁,...,uₙ}.
  2. Express each vector of B as a linear combination of C: vᵢ = c₁ᵢ u₁ + ... + cₙᵢ uₙ.
  3. Construct the change of basis matrix P = [[v₁]_C ... [vₙ]_C].
  4. Compute new coordinates of any vector: [v]_C = P [v]_B.
  5. For linear transformations: [T]_C = P [T]_B P⁻¹.

8. Applications

  • Diagonalization of matrices and simplifying computations.
  • Quantum mechanics: changing from one basis of eigenvectors to another.
  • Computer graphics: converting coordinates from world space to camera space.
  • Data analysis: PCA uses change of basis to rotate axes to principal components.
  • Control theory: changing coordinates to simplify system representation.

9. Summary Formulas

[v]_C = P [v]_B   (change of vector coordinates)
[T]_C = P [T]_B P⁻¹   (change of linear transformation matrix)
P = [[v₁]_C ... [vₙ]_C]   (change of basis matrix)

Understanding change of basis allows us to view vectors and transformations from multiple perspectives, simplifying computations and revealing deeper structure in vector spaces.
