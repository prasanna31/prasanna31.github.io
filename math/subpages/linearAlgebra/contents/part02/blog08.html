Subspaces and the Geometry of Containment

Understanding How Vector Spaces Nest and Interact

1. Why Subspaces Matter

A vector space can be huge and abstract, like ℝⁿ or the space of all continuous functions. Subspaces allow us to focus on meaningful “sub-universes” where all the vector space structure remains intact.

A subspace is a self-contained linear world living inside a larger universe.

Every concept in linear algebra — span, linear independence, basis, dimension — is easier to understand by first seeing how subspaces interact.

---

2. Definition of a Subspace

A subset W of a vector space V is a subspace if:

  • Zero vector: 0 ∈ W
  • Closed under addition: u + v ∈ W for all u,v ∈ W
  • Closed under scalar multiplication: a·v ∈ W for all v ∈ W and scalars a

Notice how this mirrors the axioms of a vector space — a subspace is essentially a vector space in its own right, but living inside another space.

---

3. Geometric Intuition in ℝ² and ℝ³

3.1 Lines Through the Origin

Every line in ℝ² that passes through the origin is a subspace. Why? - Zero vector: line passes through the origin - Closure under addition: sum of two vectors on the line is still on the line - Closure under scalar multiplication: stretching or reversing a vector keeps it on the line

This shows the connection between **algebraic axioms and geometric containment**.

3.2 Planes Through the Origin

Similarly, any plane in ℝ³ passing through the origin is a subspace. Lines are 1D subspaces, planes are 2D subspaces. Dimension naturally emerges as a measure of “size” inside the bigger space.

3.3 Trivial Subspace

The set {0} is a subspace of every vector space. It is “tiny” but still respects all axioms. This demonstrates that subspaces range from minimal to maximal.

---

4. Examples in Function Spaces

  • Continuous functions: all polynomials of degree ≤ n form a subspace.
  • Even functions: set of all f(x) with f(-x) = f(x) is a subspace.
  • Zero function is always included.

The principle is the same: ensure closure under addition and scaling.

---

5. Subspace Criteria — How to Check

Given a subset W of V, verify systematically:

  1. Zero vector is in W
  2. For all u,v ∈ W, check u+v ∈ W
  3. For all v ∈ W and scalar a, check a·v ∈ W

If any condition fails, W is not a subspace.

---

6. Non-Examples — When Containment Fails

Subset Why It Fails
Line in ℝ² not through origin Zero vector not included → violates subspace axiom
Set of all vectors in ℝ³ with positive components only Not closed under additive inverses → fails
Polynomials of degree exactly n Addition can produce lower degree → not closed under addition
Functions f with f(0) = 1 Zero function not included → violates subspace axiom

These counterexamples reinforce that **every subspace must be fully “stable” under linear operations**.

---

7. Intersections and Containment Hierarchy

- Intersection of two subspaces is always a subspace. - Union of subspaces is generally not a subspace unless one is contained in the other.

Subspaces naturally form a hierarchy — “nested universes” inside the bigger vector space.

Example in ℝ³: - Line L₁ ⊂ Plane P ⊂ ℝ³. - L₁ + L₂ = smallest subspace containing both lines.

---

8. Span: Building Subspaces from Vectors

Given any set of vectors {v₁,...,vₖ} in V, their span is the smallest subspace containing all of them:

span{v₁,...,vₖ} = {a₁v₁ + ... + aₖvₖ | aᵢ ∈ F}

This is where the geometric idea of “containment” meets linear algebra: any subspace can be constructed as the span of a set of vectors.

---

9. Geometric Takeaways

  • Subspaces are “self-contained” linear worlds.
  • Dimension measures the degrees of freedom in a subspace.
  • Lines, planes, and higher-dimensional analogues are all subspaces.
  • Intersections, spans, and sums create a rich hierarchy of containment.
---

10. Why This Matters

Understanding subspaces is critical because: - Solutions of linear equations form subspaces (the kernel) - Column spaces of matrices are subspaces - Orthogonal projections are always onto subspaces Geometry and algebra merge: subspaces are the **shape of linear algebra inside a vector space**.

Mastering subspaces is mastering the geometry of linear algebra.
