Partially Observable Environments

When an intelligent agent must act with incomplete or uncertain information

1. Definition

An environment is partially observable when an agent’s sensors do not provide access to the complete state of the environment at each point in time.

Some relevant information may be hidden, noisy, delayed, or impossible to sense directly.

Partial observability introduces uncertainty into decision-making.

2. Causes of Partial Observability

  • Imperfect or noisy sensors
  • Hidden variables in the environment
  • Occlusion or limited field of view
  • Delayed or missing percepts

These limitations prevent the agent from knowing the exact environment state.

3. Role of Memory and Belief

In partially observable environments, agents often maintain an internal belief state, representing a probability distribution over possible environment states.

Past percepts and actions are combined with current observations to estimate the true state.

Memory compensates for missing information.

4. Examples

Poker

Players cannot see opponents’ cards and must infer them from behavior and probabilities.

Autonomous Driving

Weather, sensor noise, and occluded pedestrians make the environment partially observable.

Medical Diagnosis

Doctors infer hidden diseases based on incomplete symptoms and test results.

6. Implications for Agent Design

Partial observability requires more sophisticated agent architectures.

  • Model-based agents
  • Probabilistic reasoning
  • State estimation techniques
  • Learning from experience
Rationality depends on maximizing expected performance under uncertainty.

7. Formal Models

Partially observable environments are often modeled using Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs).

POMDPs explicitly represent uncertainty in state perception and action outcomes.

8. Why Most Real-World Environments Are Partially Observable

  • Sensors are never perfect
  • The world is complex and dynamic
  • Some information is fundamentally unobservable

As a result, partial observability is the norm rather than the exception.

9. Summary

In partially observable environments, agents must act under uncertainty, relying on memory, inference, and probabilistic reasoning.

Intelligence is most clearly revealed when agents perform well despite incomplete information.
